DSN

The Digital Services Department, DSN, is the Grenoble University Hospital department responsible for the development, implementation and maintenance of the hospital's information systems.
It comprises two domains: Applications and Infrastructure.

  • Applications: The “Applications” area is made up of 5 sectors: SOMED, APPS, FAST, DILE, SCOPE. These 5 sectors are responsible for updating, adding and creating applications for the entire CHU. They also facilitate the work of nurses, doctors and the administrative team in managing day-to-day documents.
  • SOMED : Solutions Médicales is in charge of an EASILY application, a new application that manages patient records, all medical staff and hospital personnel. They have to deploy and maintain the software for the new CHU (Voiron, Rives, ...)
  • Infrastructure: The “Infrastructure” domain is made up of 3 sectors: HTTP, RS4, SCOL. These 3 sectors are involved in hardware, systems and IT support, and between them they manage most of CHU's IT infrastructure, while each has a very specific role to play.
  • SCOL: Collaborative Services, manages the entire system, from Active Directory to user rights management. It also manages everything to do with messaging and shared resources.

    • My secondary missions

  • Ticket management: On a daily basis, I create messaging tickets, mailbox increase, mailing list creation... and software application tickets, i.e. everything concerning the deployment of new applications.
  • Kate 360 It's a small project that consists of putting stations in kiosk mode and then adding just a web page that refers patients to the right places.
  • Securing Linux information systemsI'm working with one of my colleagues to develop a tool to manage the Linux installed base and automatically deploy the EDR. It's a 4-year project.
  • Log management As part of our ongoing efforts to secure our infrastructure, we are currently deploying ELK (Elastic Logstach Kibana), a SIEM that will enable us to analyze our logs and detect any potential attacks.

    Here are the different steps I had to take :


  • Inventory of all print servers
  • Search for a universal driver compatible with all models (Samsung, HP, Brother, Konica)
  • Check the eligibility of print servers still running 2012 R2 for a possible switch to 2019.
  • Create a script to modify the Samsung, Konica, HP, Brother driver and associate it with the correct driver.
  • Appointments with users + Tests carried out on their workstations (5 users per driver).
  • Deployment of the client driver package.
  • Universal driver tested in production on 10 print queues

    Skills acquired

    During this project I was able to develop different skills such as :

  • Knowledge of print servers: When I was told I'd be working on this project, I had no idea how the print infrastructure would work. I had to do a lot of research, testing different possible configurations. I also understood the size of the print park, with over 3,000 print queues.
  • Cybersecurity: I've acquired new cybersecurity skills, particularly in terms of deploying the driver for users, making sure that it's recent, that it doesn't come from a site other than HP, Konica Minolta...
  • Be aware of the length of the task: I've learned to manage my time, because you can't do just that all day, you're working on other things. You also have to make sure it works every time, so testing, retesting and all that takes time.
